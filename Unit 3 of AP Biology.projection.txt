Unit 3 of AP Biology[]Owen Keller[]7[]What is metabolism?\\\The sum of an organism's chemical reactions.\\\What is a metabolic pathway?\\\A series of reactions that takes an initial substance and makes a final product, using enzymes.\\\What is the specificity of enzymes?\\\Only one specific tertiary structure will fit into the active site of an enzyme.\\\What is a catabolic pathway?\\\A metabolic pathway that results in a net release of energy and breakdown of molecules.\\\What is an anabolic pathway?\\\A metabolic pathway that results in a net consumption of energy and synthesis of molecules.\\\What is kinetic energy?\\\Energy associated with the movement of matter.\\\What is potential energy?\\\Energy matter possesses because of its location or structure; what the matter can do but is not currently doing.\\\What is thermodynamics?\\\The study of energy transformations.\\\What is the first law of thermodynamics?\\\Energy cannot be created nor destroyed, only transformed or transferred.\\\What is the second law of thermodynamics?\\\Every energy transformation or transfer increases the entropy of the universe.\\\What is entropy?\\\A measure of disorder or randomness in the universe.\\\What is free energy (G)?\\\The portion of a system's energy that is readily available to do work.\\\What is change in free energy (ΔG)?\\\Gfinal minus Ginitial, representing how much free energy is in the system before and after a reaction.\\\What is a spontaneous process?\\\A reaction that happens because ΔG is less than 0; it is exergonic, decreases free energy in the system, and increases the stability of the system.\\\What is an exergonic reaction?\\\A reaction that releases energy, has a negative ΔG, produces products with less free energy than reactants, happens spontaneously, and is catabolic.\\\What is an endergonic reaction?\\\A reaction that stores energy, has a positive ΔG, produces products with more free energy than reactants, cannot happen spontaneously, and is anabolic.\\\What is activation energy (EA)?\\\The initial amount of energy reactants need to absorb to become products; enzymes reduce the amount of activation energy required.\\\What is reaction coupling?\\\When an exergonic reaction releases energy that is used in an endergonic reaction; the net ΔG must be negative.\\\What is ATP?\\\Adenosine Triphosphate; a molecule that stores lots of energy between the second and third phosphate.\\\What is ATP hydrolysis?\\\The splitting of ATP into ADP, an independent phosphate (Pi), and released free energy.\\\What is ATP coupling?\\\When an endergonic reaction is coupled to ATP hydrolysis.\\\What is ADP phosphorylation?\\\The anabolic reaction in which ADP and an independent phosphate (Pi) combine to make ATP.\\\What is an endotherm?\\\An organism that maintains a stable internal body temperature using metabolic reactions.\\\What is an ectotherm?\\\An organism that relies on its environment's temperature to determine its metabolic rate.\\\What are redox reactions?\\\Oxidation and reduction reactions that occur when electrons are transferred, typically with hydrogen atoms.\\\What is oxidation?\\\A process in which the proportion of oxygen increases, hydrogen decreases, and electrons are lost (LEO: Loses Electrons, Oxidation).\\\What is reduction?\\\A process in which the proportion of oxygen decreases, hydrogen increases, and electrons are gained (GER: Gains Electrons, Reduction).\\\What are electron carrier molecules?\\\Molecules like FAD and NAD+ that carry electrons with hydrogen atoms from one part of a system to another.\\\What is a coenzyme?\\\A non-protein helper of an enzyme.\\\What is proton-motive force?\\\The force created when protons want to move across a membrane from high to low concentration due to imbalance.\\\What is substrate-level phosphorylation?\\\When a substrate transfers a high-energy phosphate directly to ADP; used during glycolysis.\\\What is aerobic respiration?\\\A process that uses oxygen to fully break down glucose, with oxygen serving as the last electron acceptor.\\\What is glycolysis?\\\The stage of cellular respiration that splits one 6-carbon glucose into two 3-carbon pyruvate molecules, occurring in the cytoplasm; inputs are glucose, 2 ATP, 4 ADP, and 2 NAD+; outputs are 2 pyruvate, net 2 ATP, and 2 NADH.\\\What is pyruvate oxidation?\\\The stage where Coenzyme A is added to two pyruvate molecules in the mitochondrial matrix, producing 2 Acetyl-CoA, 2 CO2, and 2 NADH.\\\What is the citric acid/Krebs cycle?\\\The stage where Acetyl-CoA binds to oxaloacetate to form citrate, which oxidizes back to oxaloacetate; occurs twice in the mitochondrial matrix; inputs are 2 ADP, 6 NAD+, 2 FAD, 6 H2O, and 2 Acetyl-CoA; outputs are 2 ATP, 6 NADH, 2 FADH2, and 4 CO2.\\\What is oxidative phosphorylation?\\\The stage of aerobic respiration that uses FADH2 and NADH carriers to produce roughly 34 ATP via the electron transport chain and chemiosmosis.\\\What is the electron transport chain in cellular respiration?\\\A series of protein complexes on the inner mitochondrial membrane where NADH and FADH2 drop off electrons, pumping H+ ions to the intermembrane space, with oxygen accepting electrons to form H2O.\\\What is chemiosmosis in cellular respiration?\\\The process by which proton-motive force pushes H+ ions through ATP synthase, generating ATP from ADP and Pi with each ion that passes through.\\\What is decoupling oxidative phosphorylation?\\\When other channel proteins allow H+ ions to passively diffuse and generate heat instead of ATP, allowing endotherms to regulate body temperature.\\\What is feedback inhibition in cellular respiration?\\\When ATP is present, it acts as an inhibitor to glycolysis or other parts of cellular respiration to prevent overproduction of ATP.\\\What does glycolysis as evolutionary proof mean?\\\Glycolysis is the most common metabolic pathway on Earth, suggesting all organisms capable of it share a common ancestor who developed the trait.\\\What is anaerobic respiration?\\\A form of cellular respiration where a molecule other than oxygen, such as sulfur or nitrogen, serves as the final electron acceptor.\\\What is fermentation?\\\A process coupled with glycolysis to cycle NAD+ and produce a net 2 ATP per glucose molecule; much less efficient than aerobic respiration.\\\What is alcoholic fermentation?\\\A type of fermentation where pyruvate is converted to ethanol, turning 2 NADH into 2 NAD+ and releasing 2 CO2; used in yeast cells.\\\What is lactic acid fermentation?\\\A type of fermentation where pyruvate is converted to lactate without releasing CO2 while regenerating NAD+; used in human muscles when oxygen is scarce.\\\What is an anaerobe?\\\An organism that can survive on anaerobic pathways.\\\What is a facultative anaerobe?\\\An organism that can toggle between aerobic and anaerobic pathways.\\\What is an obligate anaerobe?\\\An organism that cannot survive in the presence of oxygen.\\\What is an autotroph?\\\An organism that produces its own food source.\\\What is a chemoautotroph?\\\An organism that builds organic compounds from inorganic chemical compounds.\\\What is a photoautotroph?\\\An organism that builds organic compounds using light energy.\\\What is a heterotroph?\\\An organism that consumes other organisms for energy.\\\What are stomata?\\\Pores on leaves where CO2 enters and O2 leaves, controlled by the opening and closing of guard cells.\\\What is a chloroplast?\\\The organelle where plants perform photosynthesis.\\\What is mesophyll?\\\The interior tissue of the leaf where cells contain more chloroplasts.\\\What are thylakoids?\\\Connected, membrane-bound sacs of chlorophyll found in chloroplasts.\\\What are grana?\\\Columns of thylakoids in a chloroplast (singular: granum).\\\What is the stroma?\\\The dense fluid found outside of thylakoids within the inner membrane of the chloroplast.\\\What is a photon?\\\A discrete energy particle that makes up light.\\\What is a pigment?\\\A substance that absorbs certain wavelengths of visible light.\\\What is photosynthesis?\\\The process by which plants and other photoautotrophs build chemical compounds such as glucose by harnessing light energy.\\\What are the light-dependent reactions?\\\The stage of photosynthesis dependent on absorption of light by photosystems to produce ATP and NADPH.\\\What is Photosystem II?\\\The photosystem in which light energy excites electrons, an electron acceptor carries them through the ETC, and water is split into 2 H+ ions, an oxygen atom, and 2 electrons.\\\What is the electron transport chain in photosynthesis?\\\The chain in which electron energy is used to pump H+ ions into the thylakoid lumen, creating a high H+ concentration inside.\\\What is Photosystem I?\\\The photosystem in which electrons are reenergized by light and sent to NADP+ Reductase.\\\What is NADP+ Reductase?\\\The enzyme that gives electrons to NADP+, converting it to NADPH, which carries electrons to the light-independent reactions.\\\What is chemiosmosis in photosynthesis?\\\The process by which H+ ions passively diffuse through ATP synthase to the stroma, joining ADP and Pi to form ATP.\\\What are the light-independent reactions (Calvin Cycle)?\\\The stage of photosynthesis requiring no light but needing products from light-dependent reactions; CO2 is turned into 3-PGA, then G3P, then glucose.\\\What is carbon fixation?\\\The process in which three CO2 molecules are fixed to three RuBP to form six 3-PGA molecules.\\\What is the reduction step of the Calvin Cycle?\\\The step in which NADPH reduces 3-PGA molecules into 6 G3P molecules; one is used for glucose or other production and five remain for regeneration.\\\What is RuBP regeneration?\\\The step in which the remaining 5 G3P molecules are converted back into three RuBP, requiring ATP.\\\
